Project Overview

Improve user retention via redesigning the Didi bike’s reward system

Didi bike, a subsidiary of Chinese rideshare company, Didi, is one of the three major players in the bike share market in China. They operate similarly to the Uber-owned JUMP and Lime bike in US.

Starting in November 2020, we are tasked with an ambitious project to improve user retention via redesigning the Didi bike’s reward system. The Chinese bike share market has seen dramatic growth and become saturated in the past few years. Facing fierce competitions, it is imperative for Didi bike to differentiate themselves, and look for all the innovative ways to improve their user experience. As you can imagine, this is a daunting task with a fuzzy problem space.

Challenge & Goals

How do we redesign the reward system to improve user engagement and increase MAU for Didi Bike?

Understand Reward System

What is a reward system?  What makes a good reward system?

“the key to retaining user is to create accruing benefits and mounting losses as a user engages.” - Sarah Tavel in the hierarchy of engagement

Through our interviews with users, we learned of a few reward systems that they use frequently. We examined those apps and studied how their reward systems are designed.

Insights

  1. The reward system works best when it is intrinsically tied to the product’s core function.
  2. Minimal efforts from users beside the core function.
  3. Four common reward categories.

Current Reward System

Weak presence and lack of value

A Reward system normally consists of three key parts: Action, Progress and Rewards. For a good reward system, those three parts should form a closed loop, where users are attracted to go through the process repeatedly.

We evaluated Didi Bike’s current reward using this framework through both user interviews and heuristic evaluations. There are plenty of room for improvements.

Didi Bike current interfaces
  1. Limited and weak presence. Most of users that we interviewed didn’t notice the reward system.
  2. Hard to find current rank and earned benefits.
  3. Rewards are difficult to be redeemed and not attractive to users.
  4. Badges are general and have little meaning to users.

More Research

Step back - broaden the research scope and look at the bigger picture

After our initial research on the current reward system, we learned one important lesson:  a good reward system should be integrated into the overall product experience and solve users’ problems and/or needs.

If the reward system is disconnected with the core function, the reward can end up feeling boring and disingenuous. To provide a valuable reward system to Didi’s users, we broadened our research scope and asked bigger questions about their experience beyond the reward system. We conducted our user interview and survey around two major questions.

  1. How is the core user experience of using Didi Bike share like?
  2. What problems or needs can possibly be addressed by the reward system?

Research Findings

Two main user groups with different behavior patterns.

After both qualitative and quantitative research, we understood better about users’ overall experience with Didi Bike. Unfortunately, many problems just can not be addressed with a reward system. For example, many complained about the lack of bikes during rush hours. We identified a few problems that are most likely to be solved by a reward system and synthesized our findings into the following two personas.

Reframe Design Questions

After defining the two major personas, we reframed our design questions based on the specific needs and problems of these two personas.

Brainstorming & Ideation

Brainstorming & evaluating potential ideas

Based on the design problems we have defined, we brainstormed a few solutions. The biggest challenge for us in designing the new system is to reconcile the different needs of two personas. Below are the three most sound ideas that came out from our brainstorming session. We evaluated these three ideas with NUF(new,useful & feasible) evaluation method and picked the second idea.

Message Capsule

Allow users to throw and pick up message capsules along their route after completing each ride. The capsules are location-based, so they can only be read by riders physically passing by.

Badge Treasure Hunt

Use collectable and location-based badges of various themes to encourage users to complete more trips and explore more places.

Cycling Data Visualization

Visualize users’ trip data to make it easier for them to track their biking accomplishments and share/brag with friends.

Solutions

A multi-themed badge treasure hunting system that corresponds to different users’ bike share usage patterns.

Badge System 1 - Food Combat

Users will receive a badge of food monster at the end of their trip based on how much calories they burned by riding. The food are all local food and will change per the city you are in.

Target users

Commute riders

Solved problems & satisfied needs
  • Finding psychological comfort in biking to escape from the exercise guilt
  • Hoping for more interesting things to happen in daily work life
Values for users
  • Understand how much calories are burned for their bike trips
  • Earn rewards

Badge System 2 - City Adventure

Users can collect badges of landmarks in the city by visiting or passing by the landmarks.

Target users

Leisure Riders

Solved problems & satisfied needs
  • Would like to explore interesting places and have fun biking experience
  • Would like to record and share his trip with others
Values for users
  • Find fun places to bike
  • Record trips and journeys on the map
  • Earn rewards

Badge System 3 - Special Event

Users will receive a badge of special events in different time of the year. Some special badges can only be found in certain areas. Users can also send/receive badges from their friends.

Target users

Commute Riders & Leisure Riders

Solved problems & satisfied needs
  • Hoping for more interesting things to happen in daily work life
  • Would like to explore interesting places and have fun biking experience
  • Would like to record and share his trip with others
Values for users
  • Connect with friends and collect the badges
  • Record trips and journeys on the map
  • Earn rewards

Process

Use the Hooked model/UX habit-forming model to design the solution

Our end goal is to help bike share users to form the habit of riding Didi bike and choosing it over other brands. To understand how a habit can be formed, we studied The Hook Model and used it as a framework for our design.

Prototype

Result

In the end, since we can’t launch our design, we were not able to obtain the real data to measure the success. However, we tested with seven users. The feedback we got is positive.

Ideal Metrics
Monthly Active Users
View rates of the the badge system page per user monthly

Usability
All the users we tested were able to complete the tasks smoothly with zero errors. The tasks include view badges collected, view detail information of a badge, share a badge poster to social media and find the rewards.

Qualitative Feedback
- Users we tested with like the badge system. They showed interests in collecting the badges.
- 5 of the users said that they might consider Didi bike over other bikes because of the badge system when other conditions are similar.

To be improved
- Two users suggest to make the badge system even more personal and customized.

Reflection

Look at the bigger picture
One lesson I learned in the project is to look at the bigger picture even if you are only working on a small feature. Although the reward system is relatively independent of the core app experience, we need to understand the bigger picture before integrating it into the overall experience. When taking a step back, we can obtain different perspectives about the problems, which can lead to more effective solutions.
